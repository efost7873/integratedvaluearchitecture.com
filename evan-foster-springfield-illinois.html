Evan Foster | Springfield, Illinois

Work keeps getting stuck, decisions drag, and the same people stay overloaded. I fix where that happens and who it runs through.

This page helps people connect the work to a real person in a real place

Springfield matters here because local credibility still matters. So does plain identity. Founder name pages are part of how people verify whether a business is real before they book time or send money. Ridiculous species, but predictable.

I’ve sat in rooms where work got approved three different times and still came back wrong. Someone had to fix it anyway, usually the same person who never should’ve needed approval in the first place.

I’ve watched weeks disappear over edits that didn’t matter while deadlines slipped and nobody touched the actual problem. People weren’t confused. They were waiting on someone who didn’t know the work well enough to make the call.

I’ve seen contracts fall apart because nobody could answer a basic operational question for months. Everyone had input. Nobody had ownership.

That’s the pattern. Work piles up in the same places. Decisions get routed through the wrong people. Then everyone calls it a communication issue or a staffing problem.

What I actually do

I go into organizations and map how decisions actually move. Not how they’re supposed to move. Who has to approve what, where work gets kicked back, and who ends up fixing it after it breaks.

Then I change where that work lands and who owns the decision so it stops looping. Fewer approvals. Fewer handoffs. Less cleanup after bad calls.

Then I stay involved long enough to make sure it doesn’t slide right back to the same people six months later.

Why this work exists

I didn’t come at this from theory. I came at it from watching the same failure play out in completely different places.

In the car business, customers could be asking for help and the deal still got structured to squeeze them because that’s how performance was measured.

In hospitals, people talked about care while protecting hours, reputation, and internal standing.

In local government, nothing moved without approval, and when it did, it came back around anyway because the wrong person signed off on it.

At the state level, work sat for months because nobody owned the decision end-to-end.

At the national level, I watched layers of review get added even after proving they were wrong. The structure didn’t change. The work just slowed down.

Same pattern. Different buildings.

What changed

I stopped treating this like a people problem.

The issue isn’t that people don’t care or don’t communicate. It’s that decisions and work keep getting routed through the same places whether they belong there or not.

Once everything has to pass through the same role, that role becomes the bottleneck whether they’re good or not. Then everyone else starts working around them just to get anything done.

That’s what Integrated Value Architecture is built to fix.
