Problems | Silos

Work leaves one team and shows up broken in the next.

One group finishes its part and sends the work on. The next group gets it late, half-explained, missing context, or built around assumptions nobody bothered to carry forward.

Then somebody downstream has to restate the issue, fix what was left loose, or send it back uphill so the whole thing can waste another week.

What broken handoffs actually look like inside organizations

A team has to re-explain the same issue every time it crosses a boundary. A receiving group gets blamed for an error that was already built into what they were handed. A missing detail gets caught late because nobody upstream carried it forward.

The formal process says the handoff happened. The receiving side still has to do cleanup just to make the work usable.

That is not just a people problem. That is a seam problem.

Why silos create rework and delay

Broken handoffs add touches. Extra touches add time. Time adds follow-up, clarification, and one more round of work that should not have been necessary.

One missing detail becomes a check-in. One unclear pass becomes another meeting. One bad transfer turns into downstream teams rebuilding work they should have received ready to move.

After a while people stop seeing the waste because it is built into the week.

Why this gets misdiagnosed so often

People love to explain silos like the departments just need to communicate better. That makes for a nice workshop and a very safe conversation. It does not explain why the same details keep getting lost or why one team keeps cleaning up work that started somewhere else.

Sometimes people do not like each other. Fine. A lot of the time the larger issue is that nobody built a handoff that names what has to travel with the work, who owns the next step, and what should have been settled before it crossed over.

Where this starts costing real time

A downstream team has to settle questions that should have been closed upstream. An issue gets handed off before it is actually ready. Somebody has to chase clarification because the receiving side cannot move without rebuilding the context first.

That is how a weak seam turns into a lot of invisible labor. Not because anyone woke up wanting to create rework, but because the structure keeps dropping things between teams.

Organizations love to call that coordination. A lot of the time it is just preventable cleanup.

Cross-team strategy work dies here all the time

Any plan that depends on more than one team has to survive this seam. One side thinks they handed the work off. The other side thinks they received a mess. Then leadership starts wondering why execution is dragging when the real answer is sitting right there in the broken pass between teams.

That is why I do not treat silos as some side issue next to strategy. A lot of strategy dies in the handoff.

One of the clearest tells is when people stop trusting the formal route

They text instead of submit. They walk something over instead of sending it where it belongs. They call one specific person because they know the official path is going to drop something important.

That is not a clever workaround. It is evidence that the handoff cannot be trusted.

Those workarounds can hold for a while. Then the volume grows, the wrong person leaves, or the stakes get higher, and the whole thing starts rattling.

If work keeps falling apart between teams, start with that seam.

One live handoff is enough. One pass between groups that keeps creating delay, confusion, or cleanup. That is enough to see whether the issue is missing context, weak ownership, hidden decision points, or a seam nobody ever built properly.
