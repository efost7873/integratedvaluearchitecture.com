Problems | Silos & Broken Handoffs

Silos are not just a culture problem. They are where work starts losing shape between one team and the next.

One group finishes its part and sends the work on. The next group gets it late, half-explained, missing context, or built around assumptions nobody bothered to carry forward. Then somebody downstream has to restate the issue, fix what was left loose, or send it back uphill so the whole thing can waste another week.

That is what a broken handoff looks like before anyone turns it into a vague sermon about collaboration.

What this feels like inside the work

People have to re-explain the same issue every time it crosses a boundary. A team gets blamed for a mistake that was built into what they received. The formal process says the handoff happened, but the receiving side still has to do cleanup just to make the work usable. Then everyone acts surprised that the timeline keeps stretching.

The problem is not that departments exist. The problem is that the seam between them cannot carry the work cleanly.

Bad handoffs add touches. Extra touches add delay.

This is the part people miss because each extra touch looks small on its own. One follow-up question. One missing detail. One clarification meeting. One return for cleanup. Then it happens again on the next item and the next one after that. After a while the organization stops seeing the waste because it is built into the week.

By then staff are working around the seam instead of trusting it. That is when a handoff problem becomes normal operating life.

Why culture talk often misses the point

People love to explain silos like the departments just need to like each other more. That makes for a nice workshop. It does not explain why the work keeps arriving half-baked, why the same details keep getting lost, or why one team keeps having to absorb problems that started somewhere else.

Sometimes people do not like each other. Fine. A lot of the time the bigger issue is that no one built a handoff that names what has to travel with the work, who owns the next step, and what should have been settled before it crossed over.

Where handoff problems turn into rework

A missing detail gets caught late. A downstream team rebuilds something that should have arrived ready. A decision that should have been made upstream gets pushed into the next lane as a surprise. Then the receiving side looks slow or sloppy when the real problem started one step earlier.

That is how broken handoffs quietly turn into duplicate work, repeated review, and a lot of resentment that gets misfiled as a people problem.

Cross-team strategy work dies here all the time

Any plan that depends on more than one team has to survive this seam. One side thinks they handed the work off. The other side thinks they received a mess. Then leadership starts wondering why execution is dragging when the real answer is sitting right there in the broken pass between teams.

That is why I do not separate silo trouble from strategy trouble very much. A lot of strategy dies in the handoff.

What I am looking for

I want to see where work changes hands, what gets lost, what still has to be clarified, who has to rescue the transfer, and whether the team receiving it is being forced to settle questions that should have been closed before the handoff. Those points tell you more than any statement about teamwork ever will.

The seam is where the structure starts confessing.

One of the clearest tells is when people stop using the formal route

They text instead of submit. They walk something over instead of entering it where it belongs. They call one specific person because they know the official path is going to drop something important. That is not a sign the workaround is smart. It is a sign the handoff cannot be trusted.

Those workarounds can hold for a while. Then the volume grows, the wrong person leaves, or the issue gets more visible, and the whole thing starts rattling.

Where I usually start

Bring one live handoff that people already complain about. One pass between teams that keeps creating delay, confusion, or cleanup. That is enough to see whether the issue is missing context, weak ownership, hidden decision points, or a seam nobody ever built properly.
