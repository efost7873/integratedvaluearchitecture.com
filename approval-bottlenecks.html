Problems | Approval Bottlenecks

Approval bottlenecks keep finished work sitting.

A contract can be ready, a budget change can be finished, or a report can already have the numbers in it, and the file still goes upstairs, waits in somebody’s inbox, comes back with small edits, and starts the same lap again.

The person closest to the work does not lose ownership of it during that process. The approvals move up the chain, and the correction work falls back down.

What approval bottlenecks actually look like inside organizations

The work gets done where the work lives, but the decision still has to leave the room.

For example, someone finishes a customer proposal on Tuesday. It sits until Thursday because one person wants to check the wording and somebody else wants to look at pricing. It comes back with two small changes, the same employee fixes it, and the file goes right back up the chain.

By the time it finally goes out, people talk like the delay was just part of being careful. Most of the time it was a routine decision that never needed that many laps.

Where the damage actually lands

The obvious loss is the waiting. The bigger loss is everything the waiting makes other people do.

Staff follow up, rewrite lines that were already settled, and send the same file back again. Deadlines move even though the real work was done days earlier. Before long people start padding routine tasks because they already know where the stall will happen.

After a while the organization stops calling it waste and starts scheduling around it.

Why this keeps repeating

Most approval chains were not designed in one shot. One reviewer got added, then another, and nobody went back to ask whether the path still made sense. Routine decisions kept climbing higher than they should have. Extra review got used in place of clear roles. One office ended up carrying more formal control than it could actually handle, so finished work kept lining up in the same place.

That is why the same pattern shows up in contracts, budgets, reports, customer work, and internal decisions. The file changes, but the loop stays the same.

What this can cost

Businesses usually do not take this seriously until the delay shows up in numbers. That is how they have been trained to think.

For example, in a service business with five client-facing employees, losing just three billable hours a week per person to review loops and rework adds up to 780 hours a year. At $150 an hour, that is $117,000 in capacity tied up in approvals instead of client work.

In a grocery business doing $8 million to $10 million in annual sales, a structural improvement worth even half a point of sales can mean roughly $40,000 to $50,000 a year. In a specialty grocery, the leak can be worse because the customer who came in for a specific item may not substitute. They may just buy the basket somewhere else.

Those are illustrations, not guarantees. The point is simpler than that. Approval waste does not stay trapped inside the process. It shows up in labor, delayed revenue, missed sales, and work that should have moved days earlier.

Bad approval chains usually get defended as discipline

The language in the meeting is usually about control, quality, or careful review. That story sounds responsible right up until somebody spends an hour fixing edits worth thirty seconds and the real decision still has not been made.

People know the file will probably come back again. They just stop saying it plainly. The delay gets treated like part of the job, and expectations quietly drop to match the structure.

Why the solution has to go deeper than one queue

The bottleneck may show up in one approval path, but the same structure is usually creating slow decisions, duplicate work, broken handoffs, and strategy that keeps stalling once the next step has to survive the same review chain.

That is why implementation cannot stop at one form, one file, or one approval map. The visible bottleneck is the entry point. The structure underneath it is what has to change.
