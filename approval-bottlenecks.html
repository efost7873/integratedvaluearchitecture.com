Problems | Approval Bottlenecks

Work is done. It still sits.

A contract is ready. A budget change is finished. A report is complete. It still has to go up for approval, wait, come back, get fixed, and go again.

The work is not the problem. The path around the work is.

What approval bottlenecks actually look like inside organizations

The person doing the work already solved the real issue. Then the file still climbs through a review chain that adds delay without adding much else.

It sits for three days over a wording change. It comes back over a line item that should have been settled in minutes. It gets sent back up again because one more person wanted to look at it.

By the time it is done, people talk like the delay was just part of being careful. Usually it was just one more slow lane nobody cleaned up.

Where the damage actually lands

The obvious part is the waiting. The bigger part is everything the waiting makes other people do.

Staff follow up. Rewrite. Resend. Guess what will survive the next round. Deadlines move even though the real work was finished days earlier. People start building extra time into routine tasks because they already know where the stall will happen.

After a while the organization stops seeing this as waste. It just becomes part of the week.

Why this keeps repeating

Approval chains get layered on and never get cleaned up. Decision authority sits too high, so routine work still has to climb. Roles are fuzzy enough that extra review gets used as a substitute for clarity. One office ends up carrying too much formal control, so everything has to pass through the same lane.

Different setup. Same result. Work waits in a path that holds too much and gives back too little.

A lot of this gets defended as discipline

More eyes. More control. Better review. That is how people talk about bad approval chains when they are trying to make them sound responsible.

Meanwhile a competent employee is spending an hour fixing edits worth thirty seconds, a deadline slips, and the real decision still has not been made.

Nobody says it that plainly in the meeting. They say the process matters. Then the same file comes back again and everybody quietly lowers their expectations.

How approval bottlenecks connect to the rest of the problem web

Approval trouble is rarely by itself. It usually sits right next to slow decisions, unclear roles, broken handoffs, or strategy work that keeps stalling because every next step still has to survive the same clogged route.

That is why this page should not be read as some narrow workflow irritation. A lot of the time it is one visible part of a larger structure problem.
