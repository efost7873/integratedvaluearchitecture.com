Problems | Approval Bottlenecks

Approval bottlenecks keep finished work sitting.

A contract is ready. A budget change is done. A report already has the numbers in it. It still goes upstairs, sits in someone’s inbox, comes back with small edits, and starts the same lap again. The person closest to the work owns it the whole time while approvals move up and correction work comes back down.

{% include visual-approval-loop.html %}

What this actually looks like

Someone finishes a proposal on Tuesday. It sits until Thursday because one person wants to check wording and someone else wants to look at pricing. It comes back with two small changes, the same person fixes it, and the file goes right back up the chain. By the time it finally goes out, people act like the delay was just part of being careful.

Where the damage lands

The obvious loss is waiting. The bigger loss is everything waiting creates. Staff follow up, rewrite lines that were already settled, and send the same file back again. Deadlines move even though the work was done days earlier. After a while people stop calling it waste and start building it into the timeline.

Why it keeps happening

Approval paths rarely get designed cleanly. One reviewer gets added, then another, and nobody goes back to ask if the path still makes sense. Routine decisions climb higher than they should. People who can reject the work cannot fix it. One part of the organization ends up carrying more control than it can actually handle, so finished work keeps lining up in the same place.

{% include visual-approval-cost-examples.html %}

What it costs

Five client-facing employees losing three hours a week to review loops is 780 hours a year. At $150 an hour, that is about $117,000 tied up in approvals instead of client work. The numbers change by business, but the pattern does not. The delay does not stay in the process. It shows up in labor, revenue, and missed work that should have moved days earlier.

How it gets defended

The language is always about control or quality. That sounds responsible until someone spends an hour fixing edits worth thirty seconds and the real decision still has not been made. People know it is broken. They just stop saying it out loud and work around it.
