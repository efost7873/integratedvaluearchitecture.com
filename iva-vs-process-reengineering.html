IVA vs Process Reengineering

Process reengineering usually works on how the work moves. IVA works one layer deeper, on why the work keeps having to move through the wrong place in the first place.

These categories get mixed together because both care about delay, friction, unnecessary steps, and work that feels harder than it should. That overlap is real. The difference is still important.

Process reengineering usually starts with the flow of work. What are the steps. Where are the handoffs. What can be removed, simplified, standardized, or redesigned so the process runs better.

I start earlier than that. Where does authority sit. Who gets to decide what counts. Why does this work still have to pass through one founder, one manager, one office, or one dominant lane before it becomes real. Why is the structure making the process heavier than it should be.

Where they overlap

The confusion makes sense. The category still matters.

Both IVA and process reengineering care about work that takes too long, handoffs that keep failing, approvals that keep slowing things down, and repeated effort that should never have been necessary.

Both can end up looking at the same visible problem. A task keeps stalling. A request keeps bouncing back. A routine decision somehow takes a week and three meetings. The question is what layer of the problem you are actually trying to change.

The real difference

One works on process flow. The other works on the governance structure shaping the flow.

Process reengineering

Usually focuses on steps, tasks, handoffs, workflows, duplication, and how work can move faster or more cleanly through the existing structure.

IVA

Focuses on where authority sits, what has standing, and why work keeps having to pass through the same narrow lane before it can move at all.

Process reengineering

Often asks how the current flow can be improved, streamlined, automated, or redesigned.

IVA

Asks whether the structure itself is forcing too many decisions and too much work into the same bottleneck, which then makes the process heavier than it should be.

Process reengineering

Can produce a cleaner process even if the same founder, manager, office, or leadership lane still has to approve too much.

IVA

Works on moving decision authority and structural standing so the process does not keep climbing uphill just to become real.

Process reengineering

Often improves how the work is done.

IVA

Improves whether the work is landing in the right place to begin with.

What usually goes wrong

A lot of process trouble is structural trouble wearing a process label.

One founder, one office, one role, one department, or one dominant lane becomes the default gateway for what counts and what can move. Once that happens, work that should move directly gets delayed, translated, routed upward, or kicked back for revision first.

That is when people start saying the process is broken. Sometimes they are right. But a lot of the time the process is carrying weight that should never have been pushed into it. The structure has concentrated too much authority in one place, and the process is paying the price.

You can redesign steps all day and still keep the same bottleneck if the same narrow lane still has to validate everything before it counts.

What this looks like in real work

This is usually where the difference becomes obvious.

A cleaner workflow still stalls because the same person has to approve too much.

Staff keep following the process, but the work still backs up in the same office.

A task gets simplified on paper, but still has to climb the same chain before it becomes real.

People keep blaming the process when the real issue is that nobody lower down is allowed to finish the work.

The organization keeps redesigning flowcharts while the same founder, manager, or lane stays buried.

The process improves a little, but the same repeated cleanup and approval drag stay put.

This is not just for big organizations

A founder-led business can have this problem just as easily as a larger organization.

In a founder-led entity, this often means one person still has to touch too much before anything moves. In a small team, it can mean everything keeps climbing through one manager or one admin lane. In a larger organization, it can mean one office or leadership layer has become the default gateway for too much of the system.

The issue is not size. The issue is whether the structure is forcing too much work and too much authority into the same place.

Where IVA fits

IVA works on the governance architecture underneath the process.

Integrated Value Architecture is built to help organizations handle more than one kind of value and more than one kind of decision without forcing everything through one dominant lane. That is why it sits closer to governance structure, decision rights, and organizational design than to process reengineering as that category is usually used.

Process matters here, but mainly because process is where structural decisions show their consequences. If the same lane still decides what counts and what can move, the process will keep carrying strain that belongs to the structure.
