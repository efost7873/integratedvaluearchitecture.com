Integrated Value Architecture | Springfield, Illinois

Work keeps getting routed through the wrong people.

People already know what needs to happen, but the work still gets pulled away from them, slowed down by somebody sitting farther from the real issue, and handed back after time has already been burned. By the time it finally moves, somebody closer to the work has usually had to clean it up, translate it, or work around it just to keep things from stalling out again.

That is the point where a lot of organizations start blaming the wrong thing. They blame communication, execution, attitude, or staffing because those are the parts they can see. IVA recognizes that the blame does not fall on the people carrying the burden. I start with the structure that keeps putting them there.

I built Integrated Value Architecture after watching that same mess show up in places that were supposed to be completely different. Car business. Hospital work. Government. Regulated environments. National nonprofit operations. Different setting, same arrangement. The work drifted upward or sideways, the wrong people ended up holding it, and the people cleaning up the fallout carried the strain.

What I do once I’m in

I go through the documents you already use to run the place and trace where the work actually goes, who keeps holding it up, who has to rewrite it, who has to wait on it, and who keeps fixing the same preventable problems after the fact.

That shows where too much authority and too much work got pushed into the same lane. Then I help change it. Work that keeps getting bounced around moves back toward the people who know it. Decisions stop climbing to the wrong desk. Reporting stops eating time in the wrong place while everything else backs up.

The point is not to come in, identify the most strained people in the room, and call them the problem. The point is to stop blaming people for a setup that keeps handing them bad work, delayed work, and cleanup work they should not be carrying in the first place.

What this looks like before anyone says “we have a structure problem”

Budgeting drags because the same correction keeps coming back in a different outfit. Somebody burns hours fixing grammar, tiny line items, or formatting while the real decision sits untouched. A team already knows what needs to happen, but the work still has to pass through somebody who does not know enough to make the call.

Then leadership calls it communication. Or staffing. Or morale. Meanwhile staff are saying exactly what is wrong in meetings, in side conversations, in email chains, and when they walk out the door. The problem is not hidden. The work just keeps getting routed through the wrong lane.

That matters because it changes the diagnosis. The person buried in rework is usually not the cause of the rework. The team waiting on a bad handoff is usually not the reason the handoff is bad. A lot of organizations keep disciplining, coaching, or reorganizing people when the real problem is that the structure keeps sending work into the wrong hands and then acting surprised when the strain shows up there.

Where this started

Finance showed the problem first because finance already had standing. It had rules, recognized evidence, reporting power, and enough authority to stop work cold. So capacity problems had to show up as budget pressure. Operational weakness had to show up as cost pressure. Damage people could already see still had to be translated into financial language before the place would treat it as real.

But finance was never the whole story. The deeper problem showed up when one internal lane started deciding what counts, what gets tracked, what gets reported, and what can move. Once that happened, requests got revised, reframed, rerouted, and delayed. More work got created just to keep the place functioning the way it already functioned.

Why IVA is built the way it is

Organizations are already dealing with different kinds of work and different kinds of value whether they admit it or not. Money matters. So do operating conditions, capacity limits, external effects, equity, learning, and innovation. When all of that gets forced through one governing lane, reality gets flattened before a decision is ever made.

IVA does not ask people to keep sneaking real problems through the wrong lane in disguise just to make them count. The architecture gives those domains their own standing, their own evidence logic, and their own authority, so people stop doing shadow work just to get obvious problems treated as real.

Financial

Money is real. It just does not get to stand in for everything else.

Operational

Queue behavior, handoffs, reliability, and delivery do not disappear because the budget closed.

Capacity

Backlogs, hiring lag, thin coverage, and overload are not side notes.

Externalities & Equity

Exposure, obligation, trust, and public impact do not become real only when they turn into cost.

Learning & Innovation

Adaptation, iteration, and improvement do not happen after the fact. They have to be recognized while the work is still moving.

This is not a report shop

I am not here to hand you a cleaner description of work that already feels bad and then disappear. The front door is a paid advisory call, a fixed-fee review, or another entry point that fits the situation. From there the work can move into deeper diagnosis, implementation, workshops, executive briefings, board briefings, and ongoing support to keep the same pressure from just piling up somewhere new.

If all you needed was somebody to tell you that work is stuck, somebody inside your office already did that months ago.
