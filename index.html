Strategic planning support, business consulting, operating model clarity, and decision-rights redesign

Your strategic plan is not the thing slowing you down.

A budget revision gets sent from program staff to finance, comes back for edits, waits on a director, and lands right back with the same person who made it in the first place. An expense report gets kicked back over a grammar fix or a single digit. A purchase request sits because the person reviewing it can reject it but cannot actually fix what is wrong with it.

That is not a motivation problem. It is not fixed because somebody held a retreat, hired a consultant, tightened controls, or wrote a better plan. It is a structure problem. The approval path is wrong. The decision owner is wrong. The person accountable for the result still does not have the authority to correct it.

The goal is not one more report about why the place feels slow. The goal is to change who can approve, who can correct, and who handles the exception before the same request burns another week.

Fewer returns

Budget revisions, purchase requests, and expense approvals stop bouncing between departments just to come back with another round of edits.

Faster decisions

What I actually do once I’m in

I go through the documents you already use to run the place and trace where specific decisions and approvals actually move. That can include budget revisions, expense approvals, purchase requests, hiring decisions, policy exceptions, strategic initiatives, board approvals, reporting chains, and the places where the same issue keeps coming back with a different label on it.

That shows where too much decision authority and too much work got pushed into the same lane. It shows who keeps getting pulled into reviews they should not have to carry, who keeps waiting on the same desk, and who keeps cleaning up after a decision was supposedly already made. Then I help redesign the decision rights, approval paths, escalation rules, and ownership lines so the structure stops creating the same delay on purpose.

This is not a report shop. I am not here to describe the mess in nicer language and disappear. The front door might be an advisory call, a fixed-fee review, or a partner relationship that turns into real work later. From there the work can move into deeper diagnosis, implementation, executive briefings, board briefings, and ongoing support so the same pressure does not just pile up somewhere else.

Where this breaks down in real life

I have seen grant approvals run through finance, grant administration, national leadership, and executive review, only to get sent all the way back down because nobody upstream could correct the issue themselves.

I have seen boards make decisions based on financial summaries that never reflect what teams are actually dealing with, then expect clean execution from a structure that cannot carry it.

I have seen organizations drown in reporting requirements while still treating minor spending like an emergency, turning simple decisions into drawn-out approval loops.

I have seen strategic plans survive the retreat and die in the same approval chain that was already slowing everything else down.

Why IVA exists

IVA was built from a pattern I kept seeing in completely different places. Too much work and too much authority kept landing in the same few lanes, especially finance, so everything had to pass through them before it counted as real. That arrangement creates rework, delays, and strain that get blamed on people instead of the structure that set them up to fail.

That is why IVA uses a multi-ledger approach instead of forcing every decision through one dominant lane. It is also why I talk about internal governance monopolies. When one part of the organization becomes the only place with formal standing, everything else gets pushed through it whether it belongs there or not.

Frequently asked questions

Why does a strategic plan still stall after the retreat?
Because a plan can be solid and still run into the same approvals, decision rights, and escalation paths that were already slowing everything else down. If the next move still has to pass through the same offices, the plan is not the thing causing the delay.

Why does a budget revision or expense report keep getting sent back?
Usually because the person reviewing it can reject it but cannot actually correct the issue, while the person accountable for the result still does not have authority to fix it cleanly. That creates repeated returns, delays, and cleanup.

What is a decision-rights map?
It shows who can approve, who can correct, who handles exceptions, and where a request actually moves when something changes. It makes approval paths and structural accountability visible instead of leaving them implied.

Is Integrated Value Architecture just strategic planning or business consulting?
No. It can sit before strategic planning, after strategic planning, or alongside consulting and finance support. It focuses on the approval paths, decision rights, and internal authority structure that often keep those other efforts from producing the results they should.
