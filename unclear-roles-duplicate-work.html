Problems | Unclear Roles & Duplicate Work

Unclear roles waste time in ways people learn to treat as normal.

Two people think they own it. Or neither one does. Or one person is responsible for the outcome while somebody else still has the power to slow it down. Then a deadline moves, a task gets done twice, or a problem sits untouched while everybody quietly assumes somebody else has it.

That is not a personality issue. That is a bad ownership line doing exactly what it was built to do.

How this usually feels inside the organization

People start copying extra names onto emails because nobody trusts the handoff. Meetings fill up with status checks that should not be necessary. A competent person gets dragged back into work they thought was already handed off. Staff keep trying to be careful and still end up stepping on each other.

Then somebody says the real issue is communication. That is one way to say it. Another way is that the work does not have a clean owner and everybody in the room already knows it.

Duplicate work is only the visible part

Doing something twice is easy to spot. The bigger cost is all the motion around it. Checking who has it. Asking whether it was done. Cleaning up after two versions get built. Waiting for someone else to move because no one wants to overstep. Reaching back into work that should have stayed with the next role but did not.

That is how a fuzzy role structure quietly eats hours every week without producing anything anybody would call value.

Charts do not fix this by themselves

Plenty of organizations have clean-looking org charts and still cannot tell you who actually owns a messy decision, a cross-team deliverable, or the ugly middle between start and finish. Titles can look organized while the real work still bounces around because nobody fixed the lived sequence underneath them.

That is why I do not get too impressed by clean boxes and lines. I want to know what happens when a real task leaves one person and is supposed to stay with the next one.

Where unclear roles start pulling in other problems

Extra review shows up because nobody trusts ownership. Slow decisions show up because the authority line is tangled with the role line. Broken handoffs show up because people are passing work without a clean sense of where it lands. Strategy stalls because the work leaves the planning session and enters a foggy middle nobody truly owns.

That is why this page belongs in the same web as the others. Role confusion is rarely staying in its own lane.

Weak ownership lines usually show up fast at the seam between teams.

A lot of organizations try to solve this with more meetings

The logic seems simple enough. If people are overlapping, get them in a room more often. So calendars fill up, status rounds get added, and everyone spends more time talking about who has the work instead of just having the work land cleanly in the first place.

Sometimes that buys a little temporary stability. It does not solve the problem. It just makes the confusion easier to manage for a while.

What I am looking for

I want to know where work gets built twice, where people keep reaching back into tasks they thought were handed off, where approval authority cuts across responsibility, and where the same names keep showing up as backup because nobody trusts the line to hold without them. Those are better clues than any leadership speech about accountability.

When the role structure is off, the week tells on it constantly.

Operations What Clients Get Services

One of the clearest signals is when one reliable person keeps getting pulled back in

Everybody knows who this is. The person who checks the numbers one more time. The one who rewrites the note before it goes out. The one who gets copied late because everyone trusts them more than the structure around them. That person is not the solution. They are evidence that the work does not have a stable owner.

Organizations love to call that reliability. A lot of the time it is structural neglect with good manners.

Where I usually start

Bring one task, process, or recurring deliverable that keeps getting duplicated, delayed, or pulled back across roles. That is enough to see whether the ownership line is off, whether authority is cutting across it, or whether the handoff was never clear to begin with.
