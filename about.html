About

I built this after watching the same structural problems show up in different places, under different leadership, and with different excuses attached to them.

Work gets stuck in places it should never have landed. Decisions take longer than they should. The same founder, manager, office, or team keeps getting pulled back in to fix problems that started somewhere else.

I’ve seen work sit for days waiting on approval from someone who doesn’t actually do the work. I’ve seen strong people spend half their time cleaning up decisions they never should have owned. I’ve seen teams build their whole week around waiting for answers they shouldn’t need.

That usually gets treated like a communication issue, a staffing issue, a culture issue, or a leadership issue. Sometimes those things are part of it. A lot of the time the deeper problem is structural. Too much authority over what counts and what can move has been pushed into the same lane, and too much work follows it.

I built Integrated Value Architecture LLC to work on that part. Where decisions sit. Where work lands. Who keeps carrying the strain the structure created. And what has to change so the same problem stops coming back.

Why this work exists

The same pattern keeps repeating because people keep trying to solve it at the wrong level.

I’ve seen this play out too many times. A problem shows up. People react to it. For a while it looks better. Then it comes back a few weeks later, usually wearing a different name.

Meanwhile, the same approvals keep slowing things down. The same people keep getting buried. The same teams keep cleaning up work that started somewhere else. Staff can usually see the problem long before leadership names it, but the structure still forces the same bad route every time.

That happens because the real issue is often not effort or intent. The real issue is that one role, one office, one founder, one department, or one dominant lane has become the default gateway for what counts and what can move. Once that happens, too much work follows it.

That is the gap this work is built around.

What I actually do

Not software. Not project management. Not another plan that leaves the same broken approval chain in place.

Read the real structure

I review the documents, reporting lines, decision pathways, recurring choke points, and work patterns that show how the organization is really running, not how it says it runs.

Map where authority and work are concentrating

I identify where one founder, one office, one role, or one dominant lane has quietly become the default gateway for too much of the organization.

Show what the bottleneck is costing

I trace where time is being lost in delay, approvals, translation, repeated cleanup, and workarounds that should never have been necessary.

Help change the structure

I help move authority, ownership, and follow-through to the right places so fewer things stall, bounce around, or come back for rework.

The point is not to describe the mess better. The point is to change where the mess keeps coming from.

How I approach the work

I do not start with a template. I start with what is actually happening.

Where is work getting stuck. Who has to approve what. Where are people waiting. Where does the cleanup land when something goes wrong. What keeps getting kicked upstairs that should have been handled closer to the work.

Once that is clear, the work shifts from naming the problem to changing it. Decisions move to the right place. Ownership gets clearer. Work stops climbing the chain just to become real. Fewer people waste their week feeding the same bottleneck.

Then I stay involved long enough to make sure it holds and does not slide back into the same pattern under a different label.

Who this work is for

This is not only for large organizations with giant org charts and expensive chaos.

This can apply to a founder-led business where one person still has to touch too much before anything moves. It can apply to a small team where everything keeps climbing through one manager or one admin lane. It can apply to a nonprofit, public agency, or larger organization where one office or leadership lane has quietly become the default gateway for too much of the system.

The issue is not how big the organization is. The issue is whether too much authority and too much work keep landing in the same place.

What makes this different

Most organizations are told to manage around the structure they already have. I work one step earlier than that.

The usual response to structural strain is more meetings, more approvals, more tracking, more coordination, and more people getting pulled in after the fact. For a while that can look responsible. Up close it usually means the same narrow lane is carrying even more work than before.

This work starts with a different question. Why is the work landing there in the first place. Why does that person have to approve it. Why does the same issue keep coming back even after people say it was handled.

Fix that, and a lot of the extra management work starts disappearing on its own.

Where Integrated Value Architecture fits

IVA is the governance architecture behind the work.

A lot of organizations end up forcing too many kinds of decisions through one dominant lane. Financial logic is the clearest example because it already has formal standing in most places, but it is not the only one. Any lane can become dominant if everything else has to pass through it to become visible or actionable.

Integrated Value Architecture gives distinct domains real standing inside the structure so the organization can handle more than one kind of value and more than one kind of decision without routing everything through the same narrow channel.

That is what makes the work different. It does not just manage around overload better. It changes the structure that keeps producing it.
