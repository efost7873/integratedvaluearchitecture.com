About

I built this after watching the same problems show up over and over again in different organizations, with different teams, and different leadership.

Work gets stuck in places it shouldn’t. Decisions take longer than they should. The same people keep getting pulled in to fix things that started somewhere else.

I’ve seen work sit for days waiting on approval from someone who doesn’t touch the actual job. I’ve seen managers spend hours fixing decisions they never should have owned. I’ve seen teams build their entire workflow around waiting.

That doesn’t get treated like a structural problem. It gets treated like a communication issue, a staffing issue, or a leadership issue. So people try to work harder, communicate better, or hire more, and the same problems come right back.

I built Integrated Value Architecture LLC to deal with the part that doesn’t get fixed. Where decisions sit. Where work lands. Who keeps getting stuck cleaning it up.

Why this work exists

I’ve watched the same pattern play out more times than I can count.

A problem shows up. People try to fix it. It looks better for a few weeks. Then it comes back, slightly different, and usually worse.

Meanwhile, the same people keep getting buried. The same approvals keep slowing things down. The same teams keep cleaning up work that started somewhere else.

Nobody thinks to ask why the problem keeps landing in the same place.

That’s the gap this work is built around.

How I approach it

I don’t start with tools. I don’t start with plans. I start with what is actually happening.

Where is work getting stuck. Who has to approve what. Where are people waiting. Where does the cleanup land when something goes wrong.

Once that is clear, the work shifts to fixing it. Moving decisions to the right place. Changing who owns what. Making sure work stops bouncing around or getting kicked upstairs for no reason.

Then I stay involved long enough to make sure it holds and doesn’t slide back into the same pattern.

Founder

Evan Micheal Foster | Springfield, Illinois

My background spans finance, public health, government, and nonprofit leadership. I’ve worked inside organizations where decisions moved cleanly and others where everything had to run through the same few people.

The difference isn’t talent. It isn’t effort. It’s where decisions sit and where work gets pushed.

This work comes from seeing that up close, not from studying it at a distance.

What makes this different

Most organizations are asked to manage around the structure they already have.

That usually means more meetings, more approvals, more tracking, and more people getting pulled in to fix things after the fact.

This work starts one step earlier. Why is the work landing there in the first place. Why does that person have to approve it. Why does it keep coming back.

Fix that, and a lot of the “management” work disappears on its own.
