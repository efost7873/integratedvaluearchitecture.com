About

I built this after watching the same kind of stupidity show up in places that were supposed to be completely different.

Car business. Hospital work. Government. National nonprofit operations. Different setting, same pattern. Work got pushed away from the people who knew it, decisions climbed to people who did not, and somebody closer to the real issue ended up cleaning up the mess after time had already been burned.

That is where Integrated Value Architecture came from. Not from a theory first. From years of watching the same arrangement keep making the same kinds of failures.

Who I am

My name is Evan Foster. I am the founder of Integrated Value Architecture, based in Springfield, Illinois. Before this, I worked in finance, emergency department operations, public health, environmental health, nonprofit leadership, and governance-focused research. That matters because I did not come to this by sitting in one field and making up a universal explanation for everything else.

I kept seeing the same thing happen from different seats. A request would move farther away from the real work. Somebody with less understanding would get the authority to slow it down. The people actually carrying the day would spend their time translating, reworking, waiting, fixing, or staying late because the decision came back wrong or came back too late to be useful.

What I do now

I go into that pattern directly and work on where decisions sit, where work lands, and why the same strain keeps rebuilding.

What clients get back

A clear read of the sequence, then work that helps stop the same cleanup, delay, and rerouting from becoming normal again.

Where the research fits

The writing came after the pattern was already obvious, not the other way around.

Why I built IVA

Finance showed the problem first because finance already had standing. It had rules, reports, recognized evidence, and enough authority to stop work cold. So when other parts of the place were breaking down, people still had to drag the issue through a financial lane before anyone treated it as real. Capacity problems had to show up as cost pressure. Operational drag had to show up as budget pressure. Damage people could already see still had to be translated before it counted.

But finance was never the villain. The real issue was where too much authority and too much work kept ending up. Once the same lane gets to decide what counts, what moves, what gets tracked, and what gets ignored, the rest of the place starts doing shadow work just to function. That is the condition IVA was built to address.

This did not come from polished boardroom theory

I did not build this by admiring organizations from a distance. I built it after watching bad approvals, pointless rework, grammar-level corrections that wasted whole afternoons, reporting loops that ate real work, and people with the wrong authority slowing down people who actually knew what they were doing.

I have watched budgets take forever because the same tiny correction kept coming back in a different outfit. I have watched work get routed through extra reviewers who did not know the rules well enough to be reviewing it in the first place. I have watched places lose time, money, and credibility because nobody would fix where the authority sat. Staff were not confused about the problem. They were saying it the whole time.

That is why the language here sounds the way it does. I am not interested in dressing this up into a safer story than it is.

Why my background matters to the work

A lot of people know one lane deeply and then try to stretch that lane over everything else. That is how you end up with workflow people treating every problem like a handoff problem, finance people treating every problem like a budget problem, and strategy people acting shocked when the plan falls apart two weeks into real life.

My background cuts across enough different settings that I can usually see where the story is getting cleaned up too early. I know what it looks like when work gets trapped in approval chains. I know what it looks like when regulated work gets slowed down by people who should be helping but are really just creating one more checkpoint. I know what it looks like when leadership keeps naming symptoms because naming the real arrangement would mean moving authority.

The kinds of failures I look for

Rework that keeps coming back, overloaded roles, dragged-out approvals, stalled implementation, and cleanup work nobody counts honestly.

Where that usually leads

A change in who gets to decide, who has to carry the work, and which lane stops acting like the holding pen for everything else.

What makes this different from normal consulting language

I am not here to tell you your people need to communicate better and then leave you with a deck. I am not here to nod gravely at complexity and hand you a cleaner way to describe why everyone is tired. If the work keeps getting kicked back, if the same person keeps ending up in the middle, if the decision has to climb three desks higher than it should, that is where I start.

The architecture matters. The five ledgers matter. The research matters. But none of that means much if it is floating above real work. This only matters if it helps show why the request keeps coming back wrong, why the timeline keeps slipping, why the same manager keeps staying late, or why one team has quietly become the cleanup crew for the entire place.

Why Springfield, Illinois still matters here

Integrated Value Architecture is based in Springfield, Illinois. That is not filler. This is a city where government, boards, regulated work, public-facing institutions, and local relationships all collide in real time. You do not have to guess very hard here about what happens when authority gets concentrated in the wrong place or when people start protecting lanes instead of fixing the work.

I work from Springfield, but the pattern is not local. I have seen it in small organizations, large ones, founder-led businesses, and places with layers of formal authority. Different size. Same arrangement. Too much work and too much decision weight end up in the same few hands, then everybody acts surprised when the rest of the place starts dragging.
