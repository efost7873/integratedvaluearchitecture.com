Problems | Slow Decisions

The answer is already known. The decision still has to climb.

The team closest to the work knows what needs to happen. A manager knows. A director knows. The people carrying the issue know. The decision still has to move upward before anything can happen.

That is how slow decision making usually works inside organizations. It is not confusion about the answer. It is authority sitting in the wrong place.

What slow decision making actually looks like

A routine call gets pushed up because nobody below is trusted to settle it. A meeting ends with “let me run that by” instead of an answer. An issue sits for days because the person with authority is too far from the work and too overloaded to move quickly.

By the time the decision comes back down, the context is thinner, the timing is worse, and someone below has to make the whole thing usable again.

That is not a communication problem. That is a bad decision line doing what bad decision lines do.

Why this drags everything else down with it

People stop acting when they could. They wait for cover. They hold work. They build extra time into basic tasks because they already know the call is going to climb.

Leaders get pulled into routine decisions they should not be carrying. Staff start sounding hesitant when what they really are is trained. They have learned exactly how far they are allowed to go before the structure reaches back down and corrects them.

Then somebody says the organization needs more alignment, which is a nice way to avoid saying the decision path is a mess.

Where decision rights problems usually start

Sometimes authority stayed too high as the organization grew. Sometimes leaders kept holding too many calls because that felt safer. Sometimes titles looked clear on paper but nobody ever cleaned up who could actually decide what in real life.

In every version, the result is about the same. The work stays with the people below. The decision keeps climbing away from them.

This is where slow decisions and approval bottlenecks overlap

A lot of approval delays are really decision problems wearing process language. A request sits in review because the reviewer is not just checking it. They are the real decision point.

That is why these two pages belong next to each other. What looks like a sluggish approval path is often an authority problem with better manners.

If everything important still has to be blessed by one overloaded lane, the organization does not just have a workflow issue. It has a decision-rights issue.

How this shows up in strategy work

This is one of the fastest ways a good plan dies. The work leaves the retreat with energy, hits the same old decision path, and slows to a crawl.

Then people talk about execution like it is a separate mystery. It usually is not. The plan just ran into the same authority problem that was already there.
