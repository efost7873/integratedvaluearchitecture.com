Problems | Approval Bottlenecks

Approval bottlenecks do not just slow the work down. They teach everyone in the room to expect delay.

Something is ready. A contract, a budget revision, a hiring request, an invoice, a grant submission, a report. It goes up for review. It sits. Then it comes back over a wording change, one line item, or a preference that could have been settled in five minutes before the thing ever started moving.

That is how a lot of organizations waste time while telling themselves they are being careful. The work is ready. The structure around it is not.

What this usually looks like in real life

The person doing the work has already answered the hard part. The team closest to the issue already knows the tradeoff. Then the file still has to climb one more desk, wait one more week, survive one more round of edits, and come back down so somebody else can clean up the review path instead of finishing the job.

After a while people stop calling that waste because it happens so often. They call it process. They call it oversight. They call it just how things work here. That does not make it any less expensive.

The damage is not only the wait

The obvious part is delay. The less obvious part is what the delay makes everyone else do. Staff follow up. They rewrite. They resend. They try to guess what will survive the next review. They hold work back until the right person is in a good mood or has time to look at it. Then somebody has to explain why the deadline moved even though the work itself was done days ago.

That is why approval trouble never stays inside the approval lane. It spills into planning, trust, scheduling, staffing, and credibility. People start building their week around whatever desk is going to slow them down next.

What usually causes it

Sometimes too many approvals got layered in over time and nobody ever removed the old ones. Sometimes authority sits too high, so routine work still has to pass through somebody with less context than the people below. Sometimes the organization does not trust its own roles, so extra review becomes the substitute for clarity. Sometimes one office, usually finance or leadership, became the place everything has to pass through because that felt safe once and then never got corrected.

Different setup. Same result. Work waits in a lane that holds too much and gives back too little.

A lot of this gets mistaken for discipline

People will defend a bad approval chain because it sounds responsible. More eyes. More control. Better review. Meanwhile a competent employee is spending an hour fixing edits worth thirty seconds, a deadline slips, and the real decision still has not been made.

Nobody says it that plainly in the meeting, of course. They say the process is important. They say they are making sure it is right. Then the same file comes back again and everybody quietly adjusts their expectations downward.

The tell is repetition

One delayed approval does not prove much. The pattern does. The same kinds of requests keep stalling in the same lane. The same reviewer keeps getting overloaded. The same person below keeps fixing the return. The same deadline keeps moving because one stop in the chain is carrying more authority than it should.

By that point the organization does not have a few isolated delays. It has built a slow lane into its normal week.

What I am looking for when approvals keep dragging

I want to see where the work starts, who touches it before it is done, where it waits, what keeps getting kicked back, and whether the person doing the approving is actually the right person to be carrying that decision. I also want to know who below the line keeps paying for the delay, because that part tells you just as much as the org chart does.

Approval paths look harmless on paper all the time. They look very different once you follow one live item from start to finish and total the wasted motion around it.

Where this connects to the rest of the mess

Approval bottlenecks are rarely alone. They usually travel with slow decisions, fuzzy roles, broken handoffs, or strategy work that cannot get off the page because every next step still has to survive the same clogged route. That is why I would not treat this as some narrow workflow annoyance unless the evidence really says it is one.

Slow decisions and muddy decision rights

When approvals pile up, it is often because the decision line is sitting too high or spread across too many people.

Unclear roles and duplicate work

Extra review often shows up where nobody trusts the role structure to hold the work cleanly.

Where I usually start

Bring one real sequence people are already tired of dealing with. A contract that keeps sitting. A budget process that keeps looping. A request that always comes back. A review path everybody complains about and nobody has fixed. That is enough to start seeing the structure.

Sometimes the answer is a narrow fix. Sometimes the approval lane leads straight into a bigger authority problem. The point is to stop guessing and trace it.
