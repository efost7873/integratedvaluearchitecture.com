Problems | Slow Decisions & Decision Rights

Slow decisions usually do not mean nobody knows what to do. They mean the call is sitting in the wrong place.

A manager knows. A director knows. The team closest to the issue knows. Then the decision still has to travel up, sideways, or through a crowded lane because that is where authority collected over time. By the time it comes back, the work is slower, the context is thinner, and somebody below has to make the whole thing usable again.

That is not a mystery. That is a bad decision line doing what bad decision lines do.

What people usually say instead

They say the organization needs better alignment. They say communication needs work. They say people are not empowered. They say the team is hesitant. Sometimes that is true. A lot of the time the real issue is simpler than all that. The person who should be allowed to decide is not the person who actually gets to decide.

Then everyone else spends their time preparing, waiting, explaining, revising, and trying not to get caught making a call they should have been trusted to make in the first place.

How this shows up in the room

People stall before acting because they already know the decision will get checked above them. Routine calls climb to executives. Exceptions sit too long because nobody knows who owns them. Meetings keep ending with “let me run that by” instead of a decision anybody can move on. Staff learn that speed is risky and waiting is safer.

After a while the culture starts looking hesitant when what it really is is trained. People have learned exactly how far they are allowed to go before the structure reaches back down and corrects them.

Why the delay gets expensive fast

The delay itself matters. The bigger cost is what gathers around it. Staff hold work until they can get cover. Leaders get dragged into decisions they should not be carrying. The people closest to the issue stop owning it fully because they know ownership without authority just turns into exposure. Then routine work starts behaving like it needs executive handling because nobody cleaned up the line.

That kind of arrangement burns time and also teaches the organization to stop acting at the level where it should be strongest.

This is where authority and trust get tangled together

Some leaders hold too many decisions because they do not trust the structure below them. Some teams do not take enough because they have been slapped back too many times. Some organizations built clear-looking titles and completely unclear decision rights, so everybody spends half the week guessing where the real line actually sits.

If that sounds familiar, the fix is not another speech about empowerment. The fix is figuring out who should decide what, what still needs review, and what is only climbing out of habit.

When slow decisions and approval bottlenecks are the same problem

A lot of approvals are really delayed decisions wearing process language. A request sits in review because the reviewer is not just checking it. They are the actual decision point. That is why these two problems usually travel together. What looks like a sluggish approval chain is often a badly placed authority line.

Slow decisions wreck strategy in very ordinary ways

This is how a good plan dies quietly. The work leaves the retreat with energy, hits the same old decision lane, and slows to a crawl. Then people say execution is the problem as if execution is some separate creature that showed up later. It is the same authority issue you already had. The plan just ran into it.

That is why I do not trust big strategy talk very much unless somebody is also willing to look at who still has to approve, who still gets dragged in, and who is still waiting around for permission.

What I am looking for

I want to see where routine calls are getting elevated, where exceptions have no clean owner, which decisions still pull senior people into the weeds, and whether the person carrying the work is also carrying enough authority to move it. I also want to know where people are already working around the formal line, because that usually tells the truth faster than a policy manual does.

Decision rights are easy to fake on paper. They are much harder to fake once you follow one issue from start to finish.

Where I usually start

One live example is enough. A routine choice that still goes too high. A recurring issue that nobody can settle cleanly. A meeting where everyone knows the answer and still leaves without the call getting made. Those are not minor annoyances. They are evidence.
