Decision Rights Redesign

The work is ready to move. It still has to wait on the wrong person.

This is what it looks like when a team already knows what needs to happen, but the call still has to climb to somebody farther away from the issue. The request sits. It comes back with a new question, a new correction, or a new layer of review. Somebody closer to the work fixes it, resends it, and waits again.

After a while, people stop calling it a decision-rights problem. They call it caution, communication, leadership review, or just how the place works. Meanwhile the same approvals keep dragging, the same work keeps getting rerouted, and the same people keep carrying the delay.

What this usually looks like before anyone names it clearly

A manager cannot approve something that obviously belongs in their lane, so it goes up one level. Then one more. By the time it comes back down, the timing is worse, the answer is thinner, and the people who did the work have lost half a day waiting for somebody who was never close enough to make the best call in the first place.

Or the opposite happens. Nobody wants to own the call because the line is fuzzy, so everybody keeps forwarding it, cc'ing each other, adding one more reviewer, and pretending that more eyes means more clarity. It usually just means the decision got lost in traffic.

Why bad decision routing turns into daily cleanup work

Bad decision rights do not stay inside the decision. They spill into everything around it. Deadlines move because nobody could get a yes in time. Staff rewrite things to fit somebody else’s preference instead of the real need. Reliable people get pulled back in because leadership wants one more pass from the person who always saves it.

That is how one bad approval lane becomes a workload problem, a capacity problem, and eventually a credibility problem. The original delay may look small on paper. What people actually live with is follow-up, translation, rework, waiting, and another round of cleanup after the call finally lands.

Operations gets slower

Handoffs get messy, simple work starts sitting in queues, and teams lose time waiting on calls that should have stayed closer to the ground.

Capacity gets eaten quietly

One more approval sounds harmless until the same managers keep burning hours fixing things that should have been handled lower down.

Outside pressure gets worse

When decisions drag long enough, the cost stops being internal. Deadlines slip, obligations pile up, and other people start feeling the lag too.

What I am actually changing when I work on decision rights

I am not handing over a neat little RACI chart and pretending the office has been healed. I am looking at where the call really sits now, who keeps getting pulled in after the fact, who has the authority to stall the work, and who is carrying responsibility without the standing to finish it cleanly.

Then we deal with that arrangement directly. Some decisions need to move down to the people who already know the work. Some need to stop bouncing across lanes that add nothing but hesitation. Some need a cleaner line so people stop escalating them out of fear and stop hoarding them out of habit.

The goal is simple. Fewer pointless approvals. Less rerouting. Less waiting on somebody who should not have been in the middle of it to begin with.

What gets better when the right people can actually make the call

Work stops climbing just because nobody trusted the lane it started in. Leaders stop acting as the emergency valve for every unresolved choice in the building. Teams stop waiting for permission on things that should have been settled where the knowledge already was.

That does not mean every decision gets pushed downward and leadership disappears into the woods. It means bigger calls stay where they belong, routine calls stop getting dressed up as executive decisions, and people stop confusing control with competence.

Leaders get time back

They stop serving as the holding pen for decisions that only reached them because the rest of the setup never got settled.

Teams stop working around the structure

Less backchannel fixing, less guessing, less hovering over somebody else’s inbox waiting for the work to come back alive.

This is not separate from the rest of the structural mess

Decision rights problems almost never travel alone. The dragged-out approval is tied to where work lands. The overloaded leader is tied to capacity. The constant escalation is tied to operating flow. The delayed call that creates exposure outside the building is tied to external obligations whether anyone labeled it that way or not.

So yes, this page is about decision rights redesign. But in real life, nobody experiences that as a clean isolated category. They experience it as work that keeps slowing down because the call still has to pass through the wrong lane.

Where this work usually starts

It usually starts with one repeating irritation that nobody can keep pretending is random. A contract sits too long. A hiring decision keeps bouncing. A budget correction has to go through three extra people. A department head cannot finish basic work without waiting on somebody who is already buried.

That is enough. You do not need to show up with a complete theory of your organization. You need one live sequence, a few real documents, and a willingness to look at who keeps getting to say yes, no, wait, or not yet.
