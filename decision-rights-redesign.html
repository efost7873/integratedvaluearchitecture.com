Decision Rights Redesign

The team already knows what to do. The work still has to wait on somebody else.

This is what happens when the people closest to the issue can do the work, explain the work, and usually predict the answer, but they still cannot finish the thing because one more person has to weigh in from farther away. So it sits. Then it comes back with a question that should have been settled earlier, a correction that changes nothing important, or a preference dressed up like oversight.

After a while, nobody even calls it a decision-rights problem. They call it leadership review, risk management, alignment, or being careful. Meanwhile the same approvals keep backing up, the same people keep waiting, and the same competent staff keep losing time cleaning up decisions that should have never been routed that way in the first place.

What this looks like before anybody says it plainly

A manager has enough context to make the call and not enough authority to finish it, so the work goes up a level. Then another. By the time it comes back down, the answer is thinner, the timing is worse, and the people who did the actual work have lost half a day waiting on somebody who was never close enough to make the best call anyway.

Or nobody wants to own it because the line is vague and blame travels fast, so the file gets forwarded, more people get copied, and everyone acts like one more reviewer will somehow produce clarity. Usually it just produces delay and a longer email chain.

How one bad approval lane turns into daily rework

Bad decision rights do not stay inside the decision. They spread. Deadlines move because nobody could get a yes in time. Staff start rewriting work before it goes up because they already know what the bottleneck person likes to nitpick. Strong managers stop acting like managers and start acting like translators for someone who still wants final say on work they barely touch.

That is how a decision problem becomes a workload problem. Hours get burned waiting, reframing, resending, and fixing avoidable mistakes after the call finally lands. Then people blame communication, pace, or staff judgment as if the structure did not just waste everybody’s time right in front of them.

Operations starts dragging

Work sits in queues, handoffs get clumsy, and simple tasks take longer because the call still has to climb to the wrong desk.

Capacity gets chewed up

Reliable people keep burning time on follow-up, edits, and rescue work instead of doing the job they were actually hired to do.

Outside obligations start slipping

Once the delay lasts long enough, clients, partners, regulators, or the public start feeling the lag too.

What I am changing when I redesign decision rights

I am not handing you a neat chart that says who is informed and pretending the office has been fixed. I look at where the call actually sits now, who keeps getting dragged in after the fact, who can stall the work without owning the consequences, and who keeps carrying responsibility without the authority to finish anything cleanly.

Then we deal with that arrangement directly. Some decisions need to move down to the people who already know the work. Some need to stop bouncing across lanes that add nothing but hesitation. Some need a clean owner so people stop escalating them out of fear and stop hoarding them out of habit.

The point is not to make the org chart feel smarter. The point is to stop sending work uphill just because someone higher up still wants their fingerprints on everything.

What changes once the right people can actually decide

Work stops climbing just because nobody trusts the lane it started in. Leaders stop serving as the approval bin for every unresolved question in the building. Teams stop waiting for permission on things that should have been settled where the knowledge already was.

That does not mean everything gets pushed downward and leadership disappears. Bigger calls still stay where they belong. Routine calls stop getting dressed up as executive judgment. People stop confusing control with competence, which, admittedly, happens a lot.

Leaders get pulled out of the weeds

They stop spending their week approving things that only reached them because nobody ever cleaned up the decision line below them.

Teams stop working around the structure

Less backchannel fixing, less pre-editing for somebody else’s preferences, less hovering over inboxes waiting for work to come back alive.

This never shows up alone

Decision-rights failure almost never arrives by itself. The dragged-out approval is tied to where work lands. The overloaded leader is tied to capacity. The repeated escalation is tied to operating flow. The delayed call that creates exposure outside the building is tied to obligations that somebody else now has to answer for.

So yes, this page is about decision rights redesign. In real life, people experience it as stalled work, repeat cleanup, and one more round of waiting because the call still has to pass through somebody who should not have been sitting in the middle of it.

Where this work usually starts

It usually starts with one repeating irritation that has gotten too obvious to keep calling random. A hiring decision keeps bouncing. A contract sits too long. A budget adjustment has to go through three extra people. A department head cannot finish basic work without waiting on someone already buried in approvals that do not belong there.

That is enough to begin. You do not need a grand theory of the whole organization. You need one live sequence, a handful of real documents, and a willingness to look at who still gets to say yes, no, wait, or not yet.
