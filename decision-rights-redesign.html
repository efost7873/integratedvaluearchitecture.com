Decision Rights Redesign

Most delays are not caused by bad decisions. They are caused by decisions sitting in the wrong place.

Work slows down when the wrong person has to approve it. It slows down when nobody is clearly allowed to finish it. It slows down when something that should be handled close to the work has to climb through one founder, one manager, one office, or one leadership lane before it becomes real.

That is not a communication problem most of the time. It is a decision rights problem. The structure has pushed authority into the wrong place, and now everything has to pass through it.

Decision rights redesign is the work of fixing that. Moving decisions to the right level. Making ownership clear. Stopping work from climbing uphill just to get approved.

What this looks like when decision rights are off

You can usually see it happening without needing a report.

One person has to approve too many things, even when they should not.

Decisions sit waiting because nobody is clearly allowed to make the call.

Work keeps getting pushed up the chain before it can move forward.

Managers keep getting pulled into issues that should have been handled lower down.

Teams send work back and forth because ownership is unclear.

The same decision gets revisited multiple times because nobody actually closed it.

What is usually going on underneath it

Different symptoms. Same structural pattern.

Decision rights problems usually show up when one lane becomes the default gateway for what counts and what can move. That lane might be a founder, a leadership role, an operations function, or another part of the structure that has formal standing.

Once too much authority concentrates there, too much work follows it. Decisions that should happen close to the work get pushed upward. People start waiting on approvals that do not add value. Work gets translated so it will be accepted by the same gatekeeper.

That is when the place starts creating extra work just to stay functional.

What changes when decision rights are fixed

The goal is not theoretical clarity. The goal is that the work actually moves.

Decisions stop sitting in the same place waiting for approval.

Work gets finished closer to where it happens.

Managers and founders stop getting pulled into routine decisions.

Fewer issues get kicked back for revision.

The same decision does not get reopened three times.

People spend less time waiting and more time finishing work.

This is not just a large organization problem

The pattern shows up anywhere authority and work start collapsing into the same place.

In a founder-led business, this often means one person still has to touch too much before anything moves. In a small team, it may mean everything keeps climbing through one manager or one admin lane. In larger organizations, it may mean one office or leadership layer has become the default gateway for too much of the system.

The issue is not size. The issue is where decisions sit and how work has to move to get through them.
