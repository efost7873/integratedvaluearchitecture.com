Fixed-Fee Review

If people keep saying “that’s just the way it is,” the structure is probably the problem.

A file gets revised over something tiny while the real issue sits there untouched. A decision gets approved, then quietly corrected later by the person who understood it the first time. The same manager, founder, or department head keeps getting dragged back into things that were supposedly already handled. People call that communication, pacing, or bandwidth because those words are easier to live with. Most of the time it is simpler than that. Too much work and too much decision authority are still landing in the same place, so everything has to pass through one overloaded lane before it can move.

What this review is for

Start here when the problem is obvious in the work but nobody has put the sequence together cleanly. Maybe approvals keep dragging out simple calls. Maybe the same file keeps coming back down with edits. Maybe one reliable person has become the unofficial repair lane for half the place. Maybe you already paid for strategy work and the same bottleneck is still sitting there when the meeting ends. This review gives you a defined outside read before you commit to deeper work.

What I am actually reviewing

I work through the material already helping run the place or already helping slow it down: budgets, reporting, operating documents, approval paths, planning material, board packets, policy language, hiring documents, committee structures, and whatever else is carrying the work. The point is not to admire the paperwork. The point is to follow the work through it and see where decisions keep getting held, rerouted, softened, or sent back through the same people.

What makes this worth doing

Staff usually know where the problem is long before anybody brings me in. They have already seen the same file circle back, the same decision get stalled, and the same person become the cleanup lane. What they usually do not have is one clean read of the sequence that makes the pattern hard to dismiss. That is the value here. You stop arguing over whether the strain is real and start looking at where it is being created.

What this is not

This is not a giant intake ritual, a vague assessment, or a polite consultant performance where the real problem gets translated into harmless language. I am not going to tell you there are “alignment opportunities” if one routine decision still has to climb three desks and then come back to the same person who could have handled it correctly on the first pass. That kind of language protects bad setups. It does not help anybody work better.

What happens after the review

Sometimes the review is enough. You needed a clean read, now you have one, and the organization can act on it. Sometimes the review makes it obvious that the issue is deeper and the same sequence is going to keep rebuilding until authority and work are rearranged. When that happens, the work can move into deeper diagnostic, redesign, implementation support, workshops, or leadership and board briefings. The review is a real entry point, not a bait-and-switch.
