Fixed-Fee Review

For one real problem that keeps dragging, repeating, or refusing to make sense.

Sometimes you do not need a major engagement. You need a focused read on one issue that keeps eating time, stalling work, or coming back after people thought it was handled.

That might be one approval chain that slows everything down. One team that keeps cleaning up work from somewhere else. One recurring budget fight. One overloaded role. One compliance scramble. One initiative that keeps dying for reasons nobody is naming plainly.

The Fixed-Fee Review is built for that kind of problem. It gives you a direct, bounded look at what is actually going on and what your next move should be.

When this is a good fit

Good fit

You can point to a real problem, but you do not yet need or want a larger engagement.

The issue may involve stuck work, slow approvals, repeated cleanup, recurring overload, one broken handoff chain, one compliance pressure point, or one decision pattern that keeps causing trouble.

Not the right fit

This is not the right entry point if the problem is clearly spread across the whole organization, touches multiple teams and leadership layers, or already needs redesign and sustained implementation support.

In those cases, broader structural work is usually the better fit.

What you get

The point is a direct answer, not a drawn-out intake process.

A focused review

I look at the one issue you bring forward, not the entire organization unless the issue clearly cannot be separated from a broader pattern.

A plain answer

You get a direct read on what appears to be driving the problem, where work or decisions are getting stuck, and what is likely being missed.

A clear next step

You leave with a practical next move, whether that is an internal change, a broader engagement, or the conclusion that the issue is not where people thought it was.

Examples of what this can cover

One approval chain

A decision keeps taking too long because it runs through too many people or the wrong people.

One recurring bottleneck

Work keeps backing up in the same place and nobody can explain why it never really gets fixed.

One overloaded role

The same person keeps carrying too much context, cleanup, or problem-solving for the structure to hold without them.

One repeated rework pattern

A team keeps fixing the same kind of mistake because something upstream keeps failing in the same way.

One compliance or exposure issue

An obligation keeps turning into a scramble, and nobody is clear on why it only seems to matter late.

One stalled initiative

A project or change effort keeps slowing down, losing traction, or dying after the initial push.

How it works

1. You send the issue

Tell me what keeps happening, where it is showing up, and what makes you think the problem has not actually been solved.

2. I review the problem

Depending on the issue, that may include a short call, selected documents, or a focused look at one decision chain, workflow, or repeated failure point.

3. You get the answer

I give you a direct read on what appears to be driving the issue and what the next move should be.

What to send

Keep it simple. You do not need to package this perfectly.

The problem

What keeps happening, slowing down, coming back, or falling apart.

The setting

The team, function, role, or part of the organization where it keeps showing up.

The question

What you want a direct answer on before you decide whether broader work is needed.

What this is not

Not a vague strategy session

This is not open-ended brainstorming with no clear outcome.

Not a full organizational diagnostic in disguise

The review is focused and bounded. If the issue clearly needs broader work, I will say so directly.

Not generic consulting advice

The point is to look at one real structural issue and give you a useful answer, not hand back polished filler.

Not software or project management support

This does not sell tools, dashboards, workflow automation, or extra coordination labor.

What happens after the review

Sometimes the answer is enough on its own. Sometimes it points to a broader structural problem that needs deeper work.

If broader work makes sense, that can move into a larger service path. If not, you still leave with a clearer read than you had before.
