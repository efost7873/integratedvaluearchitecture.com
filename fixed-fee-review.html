Fixed-Fee Review

If people keep saying “that’s just the way it is,” there is a good chance the structure is the problem.

A file gets sent back over something small while the real issue sits there untouched. A decision gets approved, then fixed later by the person who understood it the first time. The same manager, founder, or department head keeps getting pulled back into work that was supposedly already handled. People usually call that communication, bandwidth, or pace because those words are easier to say out loud. Most of the time the problem is plainer than that. Too much work and too much decision authority are still landing in the same place, so everything has to pass through one overloaded lane before it can move.

What this review is for

Start here when the pattern is already visible in the work but nobody has put the whole chain together cleanly. A budget keeps coming back. A routine decision still needs too many eyes on it. One reliable person has become the repair lane for half the place. Maybe you already paid for strategy work and the same bottleneck is still sitting there when the meeting ends. This review gives you a direct outside read before you spend more money, pull more staff time into it, or keep pretending the problem is still mysterious.

What I am actually reviewing

I work through the material already moving the work or already slowing it down. Budgets. Reporting. Approval paths. Planning documents. Board packets. Policy language. Hiring material. Committee structures. The point is not to collect paperwork and admire it. I follow the work through it and look at where decisions get held, where files come back down, where somebody can reject the work without fixing it, and where the same cleanup keeps landing on the same people.

Why this is worth doing

Staff usually know where the problem is long before anybody brings me in. They have already watched the same file circle back, the same approval sit too long, and the same person get dragged into cleanup again because the official process ran out of usefulness. What they usually do not have is one clear outside read that shows the sequence cleanly enough that leadership cannot wave it off, relabel it, or bury it under one more round of polite internal language.

What this is not

This is not a giant intake ritual. It is not a vague assessment. It is not a consultant call where everybody avoids the obvious thing because the obvious thing might make the org chart feel bad. I am not going to tell you there are “alignment opportunities” if one routine decision still has to climb three desks and then come back to the same person who could have handled it correctly on the first pass. That kind of language protects bad setups. It does not help anybody work better.

What happens after the review

Sometimes the review is enough. You needed a clean read and now you have one. Sometimes it becomes obvious that the issue is not one bad handoff or one annoying approval loop. The same pattern is showing up in too many places, which usually means work is landing in the wrong place because authority is sitting in the wrong place. When that is true, the next move can be deeper diagnostic work, redesign, implementation support, workshops, or leadership and board briefings. The review is a real starting point, not a bait-and-switch.
