What happens once you decide to fix it

You already know where things feel off. A decision sits longer than it should. Something comes back needing changes that should not have been needed. The same person gets pulled in again because nobody else can close it out cleanly.

This page is not about describing that. It is about what actually happens when you decide to do something about it.

Where most people start

Most engagements start with a fixed-fee review. You send the documents you already use to run the place. Policies, budgets, reporting materials, internal writeups, whatever people are actually using to make decisions and move work.

I go through them the way your staff experience them. Where does this get approved. Who has to sign off. Where does it get sent next. Where does it stall. Where does it come back wrong. Where does someone have to step in and fix it just to keep things moving.

What you get back is not a cleaned up summary. You get a clear map of where work is getting rerouted, where authority is sitting in the wrong place, and where time is being burned on things that should not exist in the first place.

What changes after that

Once the pattern is visible, the work shifts from observation to correction. Decisions that are climbing up the chain get pushed back down to people who actually know the work. Steps that exist only because nobody trusts the last one get removed or reassigned. The cleanup work that has been sitting on the same few people gets redistributed or disappears entirely.

This is where most efforts fail. People see the issue, talk about it, then leave the structure alone and wonder why the same problems show up again a month later. This part is where the arrangement actually changes.

Implementation work

Decisions move to the right level. Work stops climbing to people who should not be touching it. Fewer handoffs, fewer returns, fewer fixes after the fact.

Ongoing support

When pressure builds, old habits come back fast. This keeps the same problems from rebuilding themselves under new names.

Other entry points when a full review is not the move yet

Not every situation starts with a full document review. Sometimes you have one specific issue that keeps eating time and you want to test whether this is structural or just a one-off problem.

Paid advisory call

You bring one issue. We walk through it in detail. Where it moves, where it stalls, who is involved, and what is actually causing it to drag.

Second-opinion review

Something already feels off. You have a plan, a structure, or a process in place, but it is not holding up in practice.

Implementation friction review

Work that should be moving is getting stuck midstream. This looks at where it is breaking and why it cannot carry through.

How this shows up across different organizations

In larger organizations, work gets routed through layers of approval and comes back altered or delayed because the people reviewing it are too far from the work. In smaller teams, one person becomes the default checkpoint for everything, so nothing moves unless they touch it. In founder-led businesses, everything eventually lands back on the founder, even when it should not.

Different environments, same pattern. Too much authority and too much work sitting in the same place.

What this is not

This is not project management. It is not workflow cleanup. It is not another layer of reporting. If you are looking for better tracking, better tools, or better communication practices, there are plenty of people who do that well.

This work looks at where decisions sit, where work gets sent, and why the same issues keep coming back even after people have already tried to fix them.

Start where the work is already breaking down

You do not need a perfect description of the problem to start. You need one place where work keeps getting stuck, sent back, or pulled into the wrong hands. That is enough to see whether this is structural.
