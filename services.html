Services | Integrated Value Architecture

What happens once you decide to fix it

You already know where things feel off. A decision sits longer than it should. Something comes back needing changes that should not have been needed. The same person gets pulled in again because nobody else can close it out cleanly.

This page is about what actually happens when you do something about it, not another description of the problem.

Where most people start

Most work begins with a fixed-fee review. You send the documents you already use to run things. Policies, budgets, reporting, internal writeups. Whatever people are actually using to make decisions and move work.

I go through them the way your staff experience them. Where does this get approved. Who has to sign off. Where does it stall. Where does it come back wrong. Where does someone step in just to fix it so it can move.

What you get back is a clear map of where work is getting rerouted, where authority is sitting in the wrong place, and where time is being burned on things that should not exist in the first place. If you want to talk through a specific situation first, start with a paid advisory call instead.

What changes after that

Once the pattern is visible, the work shifts. Decisions that are climbing up the chain get pushed back down to people who actually know the work. Steps that exist only because nobody trusts the last one get removed or reassigned. The cleanup work that has been sitting on the same few people starts to disappear.

This is where most efforts fall apart. People see the issue, talk about it, then leave everything in the same place and wonder why nothing changes. This is the part where the arrangement actually changes through implementation work, with optional ongoing support to keep it from sliding back.

Other ways to start when a full review is not the move yet

Not every situation needs a full document review right away. Sometimes there is one issue that keeps eating time and you want to see if this is structural or just a one-off.

That is where a paid advisory call or a second-opinion review makes more sense. If work is already midstream and breaking down, an implementation friction review focuses on where it is getting stuck and why it cannot carry through.

How this shows up in practice

In one place, a director reviews something they do not fully understand, sends it back with changes, and someone else has to fix it before it goes forward again. In another, a team cannot move anything without one overloaded person touching it, so everything waits. Somewhere else, the founder becomes the approval step for work that should have been handled two levels earlier.

Different setups, same issue. Work and decision authority keep landing in the same place.

What this is not

This is not project management. It is not workflow cleanup. It is not another layer of reporting. If you are looking for better tracking or better tools, there are people who do that well.

This work looks at where decisions sit, where work gets sent, and why the same problems keep coming back even after people have already tried to fix them. If you want the underlying structure, you can read the IVA Standard.
