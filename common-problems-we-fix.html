Common Problems We Fix

This usually shows up long before anyone calls it a structure problem.

A request gets kicked back again. A budget correction sits for days because one more person wants to look at it. A manager already knows what the right call is, but the work still has to climb to somebody farther from it. Then it comes back late, changed, or half-understood, and somebody else has to fix it just to keep the day moving.

That is the point of this page. Not to name abstract categories. To show the patterns people are already living inside before they start getting told it is a communication issue, a staffing issue, or a problem with attitude.

What people usually see first

They usually do not start by saying, “our governance architecture is distorted.” Human beings, somehow, rarely open with that. They say budgeting takes forever now. They say the same approval keeps coming back with tiny edits while the real decision still does not get made. They say one person has quietly become the cleanup crew because everyone knows that if it lands there, at least it will get done.

Those are not random annoyances sitting next to each other. They tend to come from the same arrangement. Too much work and too much decision weight keep landing in the same place, so everything else starts bending around that one overloaded lane.

These are the patterns that usually bring people in

Approvals keep getting piled onto the wrong person

The work is ready, but it still has to sit in somebody else’s queue first, usually somebody who is already overloaded or too far from the actual issue to move it cleanly.

The same problem comes back in a new outfit every month

The label changes. The deck changes. The meeting changes. The sequence does not. Work still stalls in the same place and the same people still have to absorb the fallout.

One reliable person keeps saving the day

Nothing ever quite breaks badly enough to force a redesign, so one competent person keeps translating, fixing, chasing, or finishing work that never should have landed there.

Deadlines move even when people are working hard

The delay is not coming from laziness. The delay is coming from waiting, rerouting, rechecking, and trying to finish work after it has already been bounced around too many times.

Teams are using workarounds just to keep things alive

The formal route takes too long, so people text instead of submit, skip a step instead of wait, or go around the lane everyone knows is going to slow it down.

Everybody is explaining the problem, nobody is moving it

Meetings get longer. Reporting gets cleaner. The language gets nicer. The work still comes back wrong, late, or heavier than it needed to be.

Work gets kicked back more than it gets finished

Somebody sends it up. It comes back with edits. They fix those edits. Then somebody else wants another pass. Sometimes it is grammar. Sometimes it is formatting. Sometimes it is a tiny line item that should have taken thirty seconds. Meanwhile the real decision is still sitting there untouched while hours disappear into cleanup that should never have been needed in the first place.

That kind of loop gets mistaken for thoroughness because human beings love suffering when it arrives in the shape of procedure. Most of the time it is just a bad routing pattern with a respectable haircut.

The wrong person keeps having to bless the work before it can move

The team already knows what needs to happen. The manager already understands the tradeoff. The people closest to the issue have already done the real thinking. Then the work still has to climb one desk higher because that is where decisions have slowly collected over time.

That is where days get burned. Not in the hard part of the work. In the wait. In the re-translation. In the second round of explanation for somebody who did not need to be the decision point to begin with.

One person quietly becomes the place where unfinished work goes to survive

Every office has this person. The one who gets copied at the end because everyone knows they will catch what everyone else missed. The one who can rewrite the note, fix the numbers, clean up the deck, calm down the issue, and move the thing along without making a scene about it. So more work starts landing there. Then more. Then leadership starts treating that rescue pattern like ordinary capacity instead of borrowed time.

People call that reliability. Sometimes it is. A lot of the time it is structural neglect with good manners.

People are already working around the formal path

They stop submitting things the normal way because they already know where they will get stuck. They go sideways to someone who can push it through. They hold something until the right person is in a good mood. They rewrite the request to fit the lane they know will approve it. By that point the structure is not governing the work anymore. The workaround is.

That can look efficient from the outside for a while. Then the workaround breaks, the wrong person leaves, or the volume goes up, and suddenly everyone acts surprised that the process they were not actually using could not hold.

The language gets softer while the strain gets worse

This is where a lot of places lose the plot completely. The same dragged-out sequence gets renamed as a communication issue. Or a training issue. Or a staffing issue. Or a need for better alignment, which is one of those phrases people use when they want to sound serious without naming who is slowing down the work.

Meanwhile the pattern is still visible in plain sight. The work still has too many stops. The decision still sits too high. The same cleanup still lands on the same people. Soft language does not fix any of that. It just makes the room sound calmer while everyone keeps burning time.

This does not only happen in large organizations

Founder-led businesses do this too. The founder becomes the default reviewer, default decision-maker, default closer, default fixer, and default escalation point because it feels faster at first. Then every important call, weird exception, last-minute revision, and unresolved problem starts piling onto one person.

Different size. Same arrangement. Too much work and too much authority in the same place, and the rest of the operation starts moving at the speed of the most overloaded person in it.

What I am looking for when these problems show up

I am looking for where the work actually goes, who has to touch it before it is done, where it gets sent back, where someone is forced to translate it for another lane, and where one person or office has become the place everything passes through whether it belongs there or not.

That is why the documents matter. Budgets, org charts, reporting, policy language, board material, approval paths, planning documents, leadership notes. People usually think those documents are just context. They are not. They are where the routing logic starts giving itself away.
