Common Problems We Fix

Most organizations do not start by saying they have a governance problem. They start by saying work is getting stuck, approvals are dragging, the same people are overloaded, and nothing that gets “fixed” seems to stay fixed.

That is usually where this work begins. Something feels harder than it should. Decisions take too long. Work keeps climbing uphill. One founder, one manager, one office, or one team keeps getting pulled back into cleanup. Staff can see the slowdown, but the structure still forces the same bad route every time.

Those problems get blamed on communication, staffing, culture, or execution all the time. Sometimes those things are part of it. A lot of the time the deeper issue is that too much authority over what counts and what can move has been pushed into the same lane. Once that happens, too much work follows it.

These are the kinds of problems I help organizations diagnose and fix by changing where decisions sit, where work lands, and who keeps carrying the burden the structure created.

The pattern underneath most of these problems

Different symptoms. Same mechanism.

One role, one office, one founder, one department, or one dominant lane becomes the default gateway for what counts, what gets tracked, what gets reported, and what can move. Work that should move directly starts getting translated, rerouted, delayed, or kicked upstairs first.

That is why the same people get overloaded. That is why strong staff waste time working around the structure instead of doing the work. That is why places start creating extra work just to preserve normal functioning.

What this usually looks like in real life

Not theory. Things people are already seeing happen.

Slow approvals

Routine decisions keep sitting with one person or one office longer than they should. People wait for approvals that add time but not value. Work slows down because too much still has to pass through the same gate.

Overloaded founders or leaders

One founder, executive, manager, or department head keeps getting pulled into work that should have been handled somewhere else. The place starts depending on one person’s attention to stay functional.

Repeated rework

Teams keep revising, repackaging, or rerouting work because the first version cannot move through the structure as-is. People spend time making work acceptable to the bottleneck instead of finishing it cleanly.

Constant cleanup by the same people

The same team or role keeps fixing problems that started somewhere else. Mistakes travel downstream, but authority never gets moved upstream to stop them earlier.

Workarounds everywhere

Staff know the official route is too slow, too vague, or too painful, so they build side routes to get anything done. The workaround becomes the real process while leadership keeps pretending the formal structure still works.

Problems that keep coming back

Something gets “fixed,” then shows up again two weeks later under a different name. That usually means the organization dealt with the symptom and left the structure that produced it in place.

Decision traffic that keeps climbing uphill

Work that should be finished close to the work keeps getting pushed upward for validation. People lower down know what needs to happen, but they do not have standing to finish it.

Too many meetings for issues that should be simple

One decision turns into three meetings, six emails, and a week of waiting because nobody is clearly allowed to make the call and close the loop.

Coordination that feels harder than it should

People keep blaming communication, but the real problem is that work has to pass through the wrong lane before anybody can act on it. The place is not confused so much as routed badly.

Hidden capacity loss

The organization looks staffed well enough on paper, but useful capacity keeps disappearing into approvals, cleanup, translation, and delay. People are busy all day and the place still feels behind.

Reporting without better decisions

More gets tracked, documented, or reported, but decisions still drag and work still backs up. The reporting stack gets thicker while the actual bottleneck stays put.

Growth that creates more drag instead of more strength

As the organization adds people, programs, or complexity, the same narrow lane just gets buried faster. Growth exposes the structural weakness instead of solving it.

This is not only a big-organization problem

Small does not mean structurally healthy.

This can happen in a founder-led business where one person still has to touch everything before it moves. It can happen in a small team where nobody else has enough standing to finish the work without checking up the chain first. It can happen in a nonprofit, a public agency, or a larger organization where one office or leadership lane has quietly become the default gateway for too much of the system.

The issue is not how large the organization is. The issue is whether too much authority and too much work keep landing in the same place.
